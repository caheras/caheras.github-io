The reason we picked this dataset

Italy, like many other countries, faces significant air quality challenges, especially in densely populated urban areas. Therefore, selecting a dataset that focuses on a specific Italian city would allow addressing local problems and developing solutions tailored to the specific needs and characteristics of that region. In addition, Italy has government agencies and organizations dedicated to monitoring air quality and collecting data on air pollutant levels. This data is often publicly available, making it easy to access and use for research and development projects such as in this case.

Cleaning the dataset has improved the quality of the data, allowing for a better representation of the relationships between variables. This process has reduced multicollinearity, facilitating the stability and generalization of future predictive models.

In our new correlation matrix, we observe:

- A strong correlation between different air pollutants, indicating that high concentrations of one pollutant often coincide with high concentrations of others.
- Meteorological variables, such as temperature (`T`), relative humidity (`RH`), and absolute humidity (`AH`), show weak correlations with pollutants. This suggests that, although these factors do not directly influence the concentrations of pollutants significantly, their indirect or nonlinear effect is not ruled out.
- Variables associated with NOx (such as `NOx(GT)`, `PT08.S3(NOx)`, and `NO2(GT)`) present varied correlations with other variables, which could reflect different sources of emission or dynamics in atmospheric chemistry.

Our hypothesis

Implementing numerical optimization methods and machine learning models using historical data of meteorological variables and atmospheric pollutants from the dataset to monitor and manage air quality more effectively in urban environments with more accurate predictions. This would lead to taking preventive or corrective measures to address pollution issues and protect citizens' health.

Despite the changes in the environment, society, and technology since 2004-2005, the basic patterns linking atmospheric pollutants to meteorological variables have persisted, leading to anticipated increases in air pollution. Prediction models based on historical data need adjustments for this expected rise in pollution to remain useful for forecasting urban air quality. Insights from this dataset are essential for validating and benchmarking current models, helping understand the long-term trends in air pollution.

Model selection and validation methods

Neural networks were chosen as our next model for the predictions to capture more complex and nonlinear relationships between variables that linear regression might miss. Neural networks can model intricate patterns and interactions, providing potentially higher accuracy and better handling of diverse data distributions.

The dataset was divided into 70% to use for training and the other 30% to use as validation to evaluate the estimations of the model.  

Four different neural network architectures will be tested to evaluate and select the best-performing model. Each architecture will be evaluated using five different data partitions to ensure consistency and reliability. This approach balances the need for thorough evaluation with the computational cost, which can be significant for more extensive testing.

2nd Model selection and validation methods

Neural networks were chosen due to their capability to capture complex, non-linear relationships in the data. This approach allows us to model intricate patterns and interactions between the input variables and the output variable, providing a more robust and accurate estimation model. Given their flexibility and power, neural networks are highly suitable for analyzing the relationship between contaminants and environmental factors.The first step in the validation process involves splitting the dataset into train

The first step in the process of validating is the division of the data in training and validation sets, this is a standard procedure in which we divided the dataset into 70% to use for training and the other 30% to use as validation to evaluate the estimations of the model.  Then we use the K-fold method to improve the accuracy of the model by training it several times, each time with a different set of datapoints to use as training. 

The neural network models outperformed linear regression, demonstrating their ability to capture complex, non-linear relationships between input variables and air quality indicators. The best architectures achieved higher R² scores and lower MSE and MAE values, indicating more accurate and reliable predictions. Using an alpha of 0.05 prevented overfitting, enhancing model generalization. Most architectures utilized the ReLU activation function, which effectively modeled complex patterns, while the (10, 10) architecture used the identity function, similar to linear regression, for a direct input-output mapping

Concluding thoughts

Neural networks, though more computationally intensive, demonstrated superior predictive power and consistency compared to linear regression. These models effectively capture the complex, non-linear relationships between pollutants and environmental variables, making them invaluable for air quality monitoring and management.

Accurate predictions enable timely preventive and corrective actions, helping to mitigate the harmful health effects of pollutants like CO, NOx, and benzene, and to protect the environment from their damaging impacts. CO is a primary pollutant harmful to human health, NOx contributes to smog and acid rain, and benzene is a known carcinogen. Protecting the environment from these chemicals is crucial because they not only impact human health but also degrade ecosystems, reduce crop yields, and harm wildlife.





This project validates the hypothesis that advanced machine learning models, using historical data, can significantly improve the prediction and management of urban air quality, ultimately aiding in the effort to safeguard public health and the environment. However, if computational resources are limited, linear regression remains a viable option, as it also demonstrated good performance in predicting pollutant concentrations.
