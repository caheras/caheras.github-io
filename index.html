The reason we picked this dataset

Italy, like many other countries, faces significant air quality challenges, especially in densely populated urban areas. Therefore, selecting a dataset that focuses on a specific Italian city would allow addressing local problems and developing solutions tailored to the specific needs and characteristics of that region. In addition, Italy has government agencies and organizations dedicated to monitoring air quality and collecting data on air pollutant levels. This data is often publicly available, making it easy to access and use for research and development projects such as in this case.

Cleaning the dataset has improved the quality of the data, allowing for a better representation of the relationships between variables. This process has reduced multicollinearity, facilitating the stability and generalization of future predictive models.

In our new correlation matrix, we observe:

- A strong correlation between different air pollutants, indicating that high concentrations of one pollutant often coincide with high concentrations of others.
- Meteorological variables, such as temperature (`T`), relative humidity (`RH`), and absolute humidity (`AH`), show weak correlations with pollutants. This suggests that, although these factors do not directly influence the concentrations of pollutants significantly, their indirect or nonlinear effect is not ruled out.
- Variables associated with NOx (such as `NOx(GT)`, `PT08.S3(NOx)`, and `NO2(GT)`) present varied correlations with other variables, which could reflect different sources of emission or dynamics in atmospheric chemistry.

Our hypothesis

Implementing numerical optimization methods and machine learning models using historical data of meteorological variables and atmospheric pollutants from the dataset to monitor and manage air quality more effectively in urban environments with more accurate predictions. This would lead to taking preventive or corrective measures to address pollution issues and protect citizens' health.

Despite the changes in the environment, society, and technology since 2004-2005, the basic patterns linking atmospheric pollutants to meteorological variables have persisted, leading to anticipated increases in air pollution. Prediction models based on historical data need adjustments for this expected rise in pollution to remain useful for forecasting urban air quality. Insights from this dataset are essential for validating and benchmarking current models, helping understand the long-term trends in air pollution.

Model selection and validation methods

Linear regression was chosen as our first model for the predictions for its clarity and efficiency ​ to establish linear relations to use as a base for the estimation model furthermore it also allows us to directly interpret the values of each parameter in the model in such a way to analyze its relationship to the output variable, thusly it appears to be the most viable option to begin the analysis of the relationship between contaminants and environmental factors. 

The first step in the process of validating is the division of the data in training and validation sets, this is a standard procedure in which we divided the dataset into 70% to use for training and the other 30% to use as validation to evaluate the estimations of the model.  Then we use the K-fold method to improve the accuracy of the model by training it several times, each time with a different set of datapoints to use as training. 

Conclusion for CO(GT) Model

CO is a primary pollutant that can be harmful to health. The regression line indicates how the predicted values increase or decrease with respect to changes in the input variables.

The accuracy of 87.74% means that the model is able to correctly predict the CO concentration. An average R² of approximately 86% implies that the variability in CO concentration can be explained by the variables in the model. The low standard deviation of the R² (0.02) suggests that the model performance is consistent across the 9 partitions of the data. 

Conclusions for NOx(GT) Model

Nitrogen oxides are harmful pollutants that contribute to smog and acid rain. A regression line is used to predict NOx values based on changes in independent variables.

The accuracy of the model is 78%, meaning that it can reasonably predict NOx levels with reliability, even though it may not capture all complexities and variations. The mean R² of 78% indicates that the selected variables in the model account for a significant portion of the variability in pollutant concentration. Additionally, the low standard deviation of 0.01 suggests that the model's performance is consistent. 

Conclusions for C6H6(GT) Model

Benzene is known to be toxic and a human carcinogen. The graph shows a trend line indicating a strong correlation between predictor variables and pollutant concentrations.

With an accuracy of 93.80%, the model demonstrates a high ability to predict the concentration of benzene in air, which is crucial for human exposure prevention and air quality management. The average R² of 94% implies that the variables used in the model are highly correlated with benzene concentrations and are reliable indicators for their estimation. The standard deviation of the R² of 0.01 highlights the consistency of the model across different splits of the data set.
